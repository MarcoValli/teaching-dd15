Data breaches are information security incidents in which sensitive, protected or confidential data is copied, transmitted, viewed, stolen or used by an individual or group who is unauthorized to do so. They can happen to anyone at any time, regardless of the size or relevance of the corporations involved. The top 100 biggest data breaches in the past 16 years have been mapped on a timeline to show just how often our information is stolen or lost.

Key

Data involved in breaches has been divided in three categories:
  • cause of breach (marker color)
  • corporations’ industry or field (ring dash style)
  • type of lost information (marker icon)
A closeup of the key.

The 2011–2016 cluster

A high concentration of data breaches can be seen between the years 2011 and 2016. Over one half of the analyzed breaches occured in this time period. The losses range from relatively small (3.9 million health records from Medical Informatics Engineering taken through hacking in 2015) to relatively large (around 412 million people’s accounts from Friend Finder Networks taken due to poor security in 2016). The most sought after type of data appears to be online information, personal details and email passwords.
Closeups of the cluster of points between 2011 and 2016.

Grand Theft Yahoo

In mid 2013, the American web services provider Yahoo! Inc. suffered a security breach that exposed all 3 billion of its users in the largest recorded data breach to date. The hackers made off with names, email addresses, dates of birth, phone numbers and passwords that were encrypted with an insecure method that was easy to crack. Yahoo suffered another breach in late 2014 that involved 500 million accounts.

“The stolen Yahoo data is critical because it not only leads to a single system but to users’ connections to their banks, social media profiles, other financial services and users’ friends and family. This is one of the biggest breaches of people’s privacy and very far-reaching.”

  • — Alex Holden, founder of Hold Security, on the flow of Yahoo’s credentials on the underground web
